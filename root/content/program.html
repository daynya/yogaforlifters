    Frequently Asked Questions

  • Q: I've never done yoga because I'm not flexible. Are you sure this is for me?
  • A: The first time you walked into a gym, did you look at the bench press and decide to never lay down on it because you have never benched before? Likely not. Yoga MAKES us flexible. Being flexible is not a requirement for starting a practice. You add in these stretches to open your body. If you're already open enough, then you may not need to be here.
  • Q: I can't even touch my toes, I don't think I can do yoga?
  • A: Right. See above. There are modifications for anything and everything in yoga. Just like when you front squat, if you can't hold a "proper" front rack position (raising hand), there are alternative approaches so that you still get the benefit of the exercise and work towards the proper positioning. Same thing here.
  • Q: I've heard that being too flexible is bad for my strength?
  • A: If you are here, it is likely that you aren't practicing yoga for hours each day. It's not likely that you're going to get "too flexible" from incorporating a small amount of yoga. Just like you can't lift for two weeks and get "too bulky", it's something you really need to work toward. I believe there are some old school notions that say strength and flexibility are at odds with one another. It is my personal belief that the reality is a balance of the two. Being more flexible lets your body function more optimally and aids in recovery. This, in turn, allows you to lift more, and be safer and healthier in general.
  • Q: What if I'm not looking for a spiritual practice?
  • A: GREAT! I'm not trying to make anything about this spiritual for you. I use yoga as a tool to connect to my body and my breath. If you feel called to go deeper in your practice and want to connect to this practice on a spiritual level, be my guest. I can even point you in a few directions to explore.
  • Q: I do some foam rolling before my workouts - isn't that enough?
  • A: That is awesome that you are trying to incorporate some self-myofascial release. I am a fan of foam rolling, as well. If you feel like it's enough for you, then, great! If you're having muscle tightness or discomfort, or you feel that some flexibility would help your athletic and non-athletic pursuits, then you likely need to add something else.
  • Q: What if I just add a few stretches in before I lift?
  • A: Stretching prior to lifting is not something I can recommend. I believe that this concept is part of why yoga/being flexible seems at odds with lifting heavy weights. Stretching cold muscles can lead to injury, just like walking into the gym and picking up 500 pounds without warming up first can. If you want to add a few stretches to your training, I recommend keeping that as your cool down, post-lift. Prior to lifting, stick to a few dynamic movements to help prep your body for what's ahead.
  • Q: I don't have time for this. How am I supposed to fit this into my day?
  • A: You have as much time as the rest of us do. We all have priorities and goals. The think is, you make time for what you think is important. If you realize that this is a key to progressing your strength while caring for your body, you will make time for it. You have the ability to make time for whatever you want. If it's a couple of minutes per day, that's a great start. Ideally, you'd be able to also do a longer session on a rest day. A deeper restoration for your body will lead to a more relaxed body and mind. Who doesn't need that?
  • Q: Will yoga help heal my injury?
  • A: I am not a doctor, and nothing I am saying here should be taken as medical advice. I strongly suggest working with a physical therapist, chiropractor, massage therapist, and anyone else you trust to help guide you through your recovery. They may recommend adding in some yoga, and can tell you what to try. In general, I feel that yoga should be added in to the routine of a healthy, uninjured athlete to maintain a healthy body.
